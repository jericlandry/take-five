Take Five Pilot

What is Take Five?

I'm Eric. I'm one of five siblings who spent the last few years coordinating care for our parents — lots of group texts, missed handoffs, and some things that fell through the cracks entirely. I built Take Five because I couldn't find anything that actually helped with all of it.

You're one of the first groups trying it. I want to be straight with you about what it is right now, what I'm asking, and where I'm hoping to take it.

So what is it?

At its core, Take Five is built around a simple belief: our parents should be able to age at home, with dignity, and with some say in how that looks. Not just managed from a distance, but genuinely supported — by family, by caregivers, by people who actually know them. This isn't only about making sure medications get taken or appointments get tracked. It's about making sure they still feel loved, connected, and like their life is still theirs.

The coordination side of things — the logistics, the schedules, the handoffs — is what gets in the way of that. When one person is carrying all of it, they stop being a son or daughter and start being a project manager. Take Five tries to take that load off so everyone can show up as family again.

The simplest version: Take Five sits in your family's group chat, reads what people share, and sends a weekly summary of how things are going. It also has an agent right there in the chat you can ask questions — things like "what medications is Dad on?" or "when was the last time the aide came?" — and it'll answer based on everything that's been shared. No digging through old messages.

No new app. No forms. Your caregiver texts after a visit like they normally would. A sibling says how the phone call went. Take Five reads all of it and puts together a digest at the end of the week so everyone stays on the same page.

It also builds a knowledgebase from everything that gets shared — so things like medications, appointments, and health notes don't just disappear into the chat history. You can ask it later and it actually knows. Think of it as a memory for the whole care circle that nobody has to maintain manually.

The whole point is to meet people where they already are. I've built the integration for GroupMe first — that's what my family uses. WhatsApp and regular SMS are next. If your family uses something else, tell me — that's exactly the kind of thing I want to know.

What's live right now: the GroupMe bot live and the weekly digest live. The bot reads the group chat and picks up on what's happening — meals, medications, mood, activities. The digest goes out once a week and pulls it all together.

The bot also builds a running knowledgebase from conversations — so medications, appointments, and care notes are stored and searchable, not just buried in the chat.

The group chat, in action

The digest is one side of Take Five. The other is the agent — sitting right there in your family's group chat, ready when someone has a question. Here's a real exchange from a live pilot family, with names and details changed.

Alex 9:48 AM

@TakeFive do any of Dad's medications have an effect on his sleep?

Take Five 9:48 AM

Dad's Medications & Sleep Effects

Yes — several of his medications can meaningfully affect sleep. Here's a breakdown:

✅ Supports sleep

Mirtazapine 15 mg (evening) — strongly sedating, likely intentional. Prescribed partly for sleep and anxiety. The most sleep-promoting medication he takes.

⚠️ May disrupt sleep

Duloxetine 30 mg (morning) — SNRIs can cause insomnia or lighter sleep in some people, especially if timing shifts.

Metoprolol Succinate 25 mg (evening) — beta-blockers are known to suppress melatonin and can cause insomnia or unusual dreams. Evening dosing may amplify this.

Bottom line

The mirtazapine is the heavy hitter for sleep support. But the metoprolol (evening dosing) and duloxetine are both worth flagging with his doctors if he's reporting poor sleep quality or unusual dreams.

Is there a specific sleep concern you're looking into? That might help narrow this down further.

What's live and what's coming

The digest is just the foundation. The stuff I'm most excited about is the features that help keep your parent engaged — not just monitored. There's a lot of research on how much daily engagement and social connection actually matter for cognitive health and longevity, and I want Take Five to help with that, not just log what's happening.

Life Log. live A running record of what she's reading, watching, doing — not just health updates. Books finished, shows she loved, walks taken. A picture of a life still being lived, and a quiet signal when engagement starts to slip.
Pre-visit appointment summary. live Before a doctor visit, Take Five generates a concise briefing: recent mood and sleep patterns, current medications, anything flagged in the chat over the past few weeks, and a short list of questions worth raising with the doctor. No more scrambling to remember what happened last month.
Post-visit extraction. coming After an appointment, drop the notes or a summary into the group chat and Take Five pulls out what matters — decisions made, next steps, and who's responsible for what. It logs it to the care record automatically so nothing disappears into scroll.
Clinical signal detection. coming Take Five watches for health patterns in the chat and flags things worth a closer look — a symptom mentioned once and then dropped, a change in energy or mood over several weeks, a fall risk signal buried in normal conversation. Things that are easy to miss in the day-to-day but matter a lot over time.
Conversation starters. coming Based on what she's been into lately, Take Five could suggest things to bring up on your next call. Small prompts that make it easier to connect — and help her feel like people are actually paying attention.
Decision support. coming When the hard questions come up — do we need to bring in a home care aide, is it time to look at a memory care facility — Take Five will help the family think it through together. Trend data, context from the care history, and a way to have that conversation without everyone operating off different information.
The Story. coming An ongoing memoir interview — one gentle question a week — that builds into her life in her own words over time. We're testing interest in this one; if it resonates with your families, it moves up the list.

If any of those land differently than the others, say so. That helps me figure out what to build next.
