The science

Social connection
is healthcare.

The research is unambiguous. Keeping your parent connected — to family, to stories, to life — is one of the most powerful health interventions available. Take Five is built on this science.

29%

Increased risk of premature death from social isolation — comparable to smoking or obesity (National Academies, 2020)

50%

Increased risk of dementia among older adults experiencing chronic loneliness or isolation (HHS, 2023)

15

Cigarettes per day — the equivalent mortality impact of being socially disconnected (U.S. Surgeon General, 2023)

1 in 2

U.S. adults report measurable loneliness — declared a public health epidemic (Surgeon General's Advisory, 2023)

"Social connection is a significant predictor of longevity and better physical, cognitive, and mental health, while social isolation and loneliness are significant predictors of premature death and poor health."

U.S. Surgeon General's Advisory on the Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation (2023) · National Academies of Sciences, Engineering & Medicine · World Health Organization Commission on Social Connection

The problem

Coordination collapse forces people
out of their homes.

People end up in managed care not because they have to — but because the logistics became unmanageable, and the connections that keep them healthy were allowed to fade.

01
One person carries everything

One exhausted adult child manages schedules, meds, and decisions — often from a distance, alongside siblings with varying involvement.

02
Tasks fall through the cracks

No shared visibility means no shared accountability. Things get missed. Crises happen that didn't have to.

03
Connection slowly fades

As logistics take over, the human relationship suffers. The visits get shorter. The isolation grows — quietly, dangerously, with real health consequences.

04
Stories are lost forever

Every person over 80 is a living archive. Without someone asking the questions, those stories disappear when they do.

How it works

Simple for everyone.
Powerful for families.

No apps. No training. No friction. Just a phone number and a calm, intelligent presence holding everything together.

1
Set up your care circle

Invite family, professional caregivers, and anyone in your parent's support network. Each person gets role-appropriate visibility.

2
Caregivers check in by text

After every visit, caregivers text naturally — "She ate well, loved her book, seemed happy today." No app. No form. Just a message.

3
The agent holds it all

Take Five parses every update, maintains the care log and life log, assigns tasks, and surfaces what matters — before it becomes a crisis.

4
Family gets a daily digest

Every morning: what happened, what she's reading, what needs a decision. One tap to respond. The child in Denver feels present.

5
The Story grows over time

Each week the agent asks one gentle question. The answers build into a memoir — her life, in her own words, preserved forever.

6
Hard decisions, made together

When the hardest question comes, the agent gives your whole family the same data, the same guidance, and a way to decide together with dignity.

The Life Log

Not just what she needs.
What she loves.

Most care apps track what's wrong. Take Five tracks what's alive. Research shows that engagement in daily life — books, shows, conversations, movement — is one of the most powerful protections against cognitive decline and premature mortality.

Books & reading

Currently reading, just finished, loved or didn't — the reading life tracked naturally through caregiver check-ins.

Shows & films

What she's watching, what made her laugh — so the next caregiver already has something to talk about.

Activities & movement

Walks, garden time, phone calls with grandkids. Small signals of vitality — and early warnings when they stop.

Mood & good days

A simple daily signal. One tap from the caregiver. Over time the most valuable dataset in the product.

The Story

Every person is
a living archive.
Ask the questions.

Take Five conducts an ongoing, gentle interview — one question a week — that builds into a memoir over time. Her life, in her own words. The stories that would otherwise disappear.

Research on reminiscence therapy shows that life review is clinically beneficial for aging adults — reducing depression, increasing sense of purpose, and strengthening family bonds. The Story is therapy, legacy, and connection in a single feature.

What do you remember about the house you grew up in — what did it smell like?
What was the moment you knew he was the one?
What do you want the grandchildren to know about who you really are?
